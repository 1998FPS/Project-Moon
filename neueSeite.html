The Moon

Orbiting patiently, a guardian of the night.

Did you know the Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and has been our silent companion for over 4.5 billion years? Despite its calm appearance, the Moon is full of surprises! Its surface is covered with craters from countless cosmic collisions, and the gray dust you see is called regolith.

One of the coolest facts: the Moon is slowly drifting away from Earth—about 3.8 centimeters per year. That means millions of years ago, the Moon was much closer, making our nights brighter!

Ever wondered why we always see the same side of the Moon? That’s because of tidal locking—the Moon rotates at the exact same speed it orbits Earth. This gives us a “near side” full of familiar craters and a mysterious “far side” we never see from here.

And here’s a fun mind-bender: footprints left by astronauts during the Apollo missions could stay there for millions of years, because the Moon has no wind or rain to wash them away.

The Moon isn’t just a pretty night-time spectacle—it has inspired myths, guided explorers, and even influences the ocean tides. Next time you look up, imagine the Moon silently watching over Earth, keeping secrets in its silent, cratered landscapes.

The Legacy of Apollo

The Apollo program, launched by NASA in the 1960s, achieved humanity’s first steps on the Moon. Apollo 11’s historic landing in 1969 marked a giant leap for mankind, with astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walking on the lunar surface while Michael Collins orbited above. The missions brought back hundreds of kilograms of Moon rocks, expanded our understanding of the Moon’s geology, and inspired generations to look beyond our planet.
